# EXPANSION_ATLAS.md — Open Lightly

This document is the strategic thinking space for Open Lightly's long-range product vision. It lives alongside PROJECT_SCOPE.md but serves a different function: where the scope document answers "what are we building now," this document asks "who are we building for next, and what does the product become when we get there." It should be read before making any significant product direction decision. It should be updated whenever the strategic understanding of the market or the user shifts. Nothing here overrides PROJECT_SCOPE.md — it extends it.

**Last Updated:** March 20, 2026

---

## 1. The Three-Act Strategy — Full Map

The three-act structure is not a release calendar. It is a trust-building sequence. Each act earns the right to make a broader claim about who the product serves. Acting out of sequence — marketing to Act 3 users before Act 1 is proven — would make the product feel unfocused to everyone. The sequence exists because credibility compounds the same way data does.

---

### Act 1 — The Launch: New NM Couples

**Who they are**

Two people in a committed relationship who have had, or are about to have, the conversation. One partner usually initiated. They're not in crisis — they're in the gap. The gap between "we're curious about this" and "we've actually talked about what it would mean for us." That gap is where most NM journeys end before they start, not because people don't want it but because they don't have a structured way to have the conversation without it feeling like an accusation, a test, or a referendum on the relationship.

They are not the stereotype. They are a software engineer and a nurse who have been together seven years and read an article. They are a couple in their late twenties who noticed they both responded to something on Instagram. They are people who have good relationships and want to explore without destroying what they have. They are scared and curious in equal measure.

**The specific moment being served**

The app enters at the moment of highest intent and highest anxiety — when curiosity has crystallized into "we should probably actually talk about this." Before that moment, there's nothing to act on. After it, the conversation either happens or gets indefinitely deferred. Open Lightly serves the moment when both partners are ready to try but neither knows how to start.

This moment is time-bounded. It doesn't last forever. That's what makes it a front door rather than a long-term retention hook by itself — and why the product needs Acts 2 and 3 to sustain itself.

**Why this is the right front door**

The acute problem / immediate relief / paywall mechanic works best here. The pain is clear. The desire for structure is high. The Desire Map mutual reveal produces a result so personally specific that the paywall lands at peak intent.

There's also a credibility reason. The most skeptical version of "who is this app for" is answered cleanly by Act 1: it's for couples in the hardest moment of the earliest stage of NM exploration. That's a defensible, comprehensible market. The App Store reviewer understands it. The journalist covering it understands it. The friend recommending it can explain it in one sentence. Building credibility here makes every subsequent act easier to market.

**What success looks like before Act 2 begins**

- D30 retention above 40% for couples who completed the Desire Map reveal
- At least one Roadmap stage completed per couple before they churn
- Net Promoter Score among Act 1 users sufficient to generate word-of-mouth to experienced practitioners (the Act 2 trigger)
- Enough pattern data from Act 1 users that the Insight Engine has something meaningful to surface

The signal that Act 1 has worked is not downloads. It's that couples are returning after their first session — that the app has a reason to be opened again after the initial reveal. If couples complete the Desire Map, have the conversation, and never open the app again, Act 1 has failed as a retention story even if it succeeded as a conversion story.

**The natural graduation point**

Act 1 users graduate into Act 2 when they've done the exploration work and moved from "figuring it out" to "doing it." That transition is not marked by any single event but by a cluster: they've worked through most of the Coupled Curious roadmap, they've had real experiences (not just hypothetical ones), and the questions they're asking have shifted from "can we do this?" to "how do we sustain this well?" When the tone of their sessions changes — from cautious exploration to operational navigation — they've graduated.

The product should recognize this shift and surface the Act 2 tools naturally, not as a marketing campaign but as a contextual recommendation. "You've been doing this a while. Here are some tools for the longer road."

---

### Act 2 — The First Expansion: ENM Practitioners

**Who arrives in Act 2 and why**

Two distinct populations arrive simultaneously.

The first is Act 1 graduates — couples who worked through the Curious roadmap, moved into practice, and now need different tools. They don't need to be sold anything. They already trust the product. They need it to grow with them.

The second is the more strategically interesting population: experienced practitioners who download the app because someone recommended it or because they read about the mutual reveal mechanic. They've been doing ENM for years — some for decades. They have their own systems, their own agreements, their own language. They downloaded skeptically.

What this second population discovers is not what they expected. They expected a beginner's guide. They found infrastructure they've never had. The Agreements Vault is the thing they've been keeping in a Google Doc. The Jealousy Mapping tool is the thing they've been trying to do in their heads. The daily pulse is the check-in they've been meaning to do with their partners but never formalized. The "aha" is: *this was built for where we are, not just for where we started.*

**Why Act 2 feels like expansion rather than pivot**

The product identity doesn't change. The card decks, the mutual reveal, the conversation framework — these are still the core. What Act 2 adds is depth behind the same front door. Experienced practitioners don't need different onboarding philosophy; they need different tools once they're inside.

The framing shift is subtle but important: Act 1 asks "what do you want?" Act 2 asks "how do you sustain what you have?" The emotional register shifts from exploratory to operational. The product accommodates this by surfacing Act 2 tools as users demonstrate experience, not by creating a separate product.

**The marketing shift**

Act 1 marketing speaks to the moment of first conversation. Act 2 marketing speaks to a way of life. The tagline changes from "the tool couples have been looking for since the first conversation they couldn't finish" to "for people doing non-monogamy intentionally." That second phrase reaches experienced practitioners in a way the first doesn't — it respects their fluency instead of assuming they need to start from scratch.

This shift in marketing language does not require a new App Store listing or a rebrand. It requires a shift in where and how the product is talked about — which communities, which podcasts, which influencers. The product itself is ready. The story just needs to grow.

**What success looks like before Act 3 begins**

- Act 2 features (Pulse, Agreements Vault, Jealousy Mapping) have meaningful engagement from both graduated Act 1 users and newly downloaded experienced practitioners
- LTV for Act 2 users demonstrably higher than Act 1 users (as expected, given higher ongoing-use features)
- The product has established credibility in ENM community spaces — positive reviews on ENM forums, podcast mentions, community recommendations
- Solo users are arriving organically and finding a path through the product despite not being the primary marketing target

The readiness signal for Act 3 is not a feature milestone — it's a community signal. When solo ENM practitioners are talking about the app in solo poly spaces and expressing either "I wish this was more for me" or "this actually works for me even though it's built for couples," it's time to make the claim explicitly.

---

### Act 3 — The Second Expansion: Solo Users

**Who arrives in Act 3 and why**

Solo users have been present in the architecture since V1.0. What changes in Act 3 is that the product publicly claims them. The two populations arriving:

The first: solo poly people with established practices who found the app through word of mouth and navigated it despite it being couple-framed. They've been using the Roadmap, the Reflection tools, the self-knowledge features. They've been getting value. They've also felt slightly like guests at someone else's dinner party.

The second: NM-curious solo people who don't yet have a partner to explore with. They want to do the self-work before the relationship, not during it. They're the person who reads three books about polyamory before downloading the app. They're preparing.

**The reframe that makes Act 3 coherent**

The Act 3 reframe is the most important strategic thought in this document: this was never about having a partner. It was always about doing the work of non-monogamy intentionally. The couples framing wasn't the product's identity — it was the front door for Act 1. The actual product — structured self-reflection, mutual transparency tools, emotional logging, the commitment to doing this thoughtfully — serves anyone doing this kind of work, regardless of relationship structure.

This reframe has to feel like a revelation, not a pivot. If it's positioned as "we've added solo features," it feels like an afterthought. If it's positioned as "we've always been this — solo users just get to hear it now," it feels inevitable. The difference is entirely in how the story is told.

**Why solo users were always going to belong here**

The Desire Map works as a solo tool. Rating 17 items privately, understanding your own clarity on each, knowing what you'd want to share and what you'd want to protect — this is useful before there's a partner to share it with. The mutual reveal is still the payoff, but the solo completion is already valuable. A solo user who uses the Desire Map to get clear on their own desires before entering a new relationship has used the app exactly as intended.

The Roadmap, the Reflection tools, the Jealousy Mapping, the Daily Pulse — every Act 2 feature works for a solo practitioner with multiple connections. The Connection Cards work even better, because solo poly people often have more complex relational networks to track.

Solo users were always going to belong here because the core product — intentional navigation of non-monogamy — doesn't require a primary partner. It requires intention.

**The Desire Map solo mode**

The solo Desire Map completion should be reframed at the UI level in Act 3 as a personal clarity tool. The flow: rate all 17 items privately, receive a summary of your own clarity and alignment (where do you feel certain? where do you feel ambiguous?), store this as a personal document that can be shared when ready. The mutual reveal mechanic is preserved — solo users can invite a partner to complete their own map when the time comes. But the solo completion stands on its own as a meaningful act of self-knowledge.

This is the product's clearest signal to solo users that they belong. Not a marketing message — a feature that treats their use case as complete.

**The marketing shift**

"For people who take relationships seriously. All kinds of relationships."

This is the destination the product has been building toward. It's true at Act 1 — couples taking their exploration seriously. It's true at Act 2 — practitioners taking their practice seriously. It's fully true at Act 3 — solo people taking every relationship in their network seriously, including the one with themselves.

The tagline doesn't require anyone to identify as ENM. It doesn't require a partner. It requires only the disposition to be thoughtful. That disposition is the product's actual market.

## 2. User Universe — Everyone This Product Can Serve

The twelve user types below are mapped to acts, features, and retention mechanics. This is not exhaustive — it's the minimum useful map. New user types should be added as they're confirmed by usage data.

---

### New Couple, Curious, No Experience

**Who:** Together 2–10 years. One or both have recently become curious about ENM. No prior experience. The conversation hasn't happened yet or happened once and stalled.

**Primary pain:** "I want to bring this up but I don't know how to start without it feeling like there's something wrong with our relationship."

**Act:** 1 — Front door

**Feature that serves them best:** The Desire Map mutual reveal. The first glimpse of alignment (or honest misalignment) is the product-defining moment for this user.

**Path through the product:** Onboarding → Coupled Curious roadmap → Desire Map → Stage 1–3 card decks → Readiness Assessment → Stage 4+ as they move forward

**Why they stay:** Momentum. Each completed stage unlocks the next, and the pre/post processing cards give them something to do between sessions. The data they've logged becomes a shared artifact — the history of their exploration. Leaving means losing that record.

---

### New Couple, One Partner More Ready Than the Other

**Who:** Same baseline as above but with asymmetric enthusiasm. One partner has done a lot of reading and is more ready. The other is uncertain, possibly anxious, possibly accommodating but not genuinely aligned.

**Primary pain:** "I feel like I'm being brought along rather than choosing this. I don't know how to say that without it seeming like I'm blocking my partner."

**Act:** 1 — Front door, but requires careful content design

**Feature that serves them best:** The Solo Reflection gate. The fact that each person completes their reflection independently before any shared content ensures that both partners have space to be honest with themselves before performing for each other.

**Path through the product:** Solo Reflection → individual archetype classification → Coupled content only when both have completed private work → deliberate pacing on Stage 1–2 content

**Why they stay:** The more hesitant partner stays because the app never pressures. Every "Not Ready" tap is validated. Every skip is frictionless. The app doesn't take sides. If the reluctant partner feels consistently respected by the product experience, they stay long enough to find their own curiosity.

**Note:** This user type is also a churn risk if the asymmetry is severe and the relationship doesn't resolve it. The product can't fix that — but it can ensure it doesn't make it worse.

---

### Established Couple, Newly Opening After Years Together

**Who:** Together 7–20+ years. Long-term partnership, possibly married. The NM conversation is new for them even if the relationship isn't. High stakes. Often have children, shared finances, shared community — the entanglement makes exploration feel riskier.

**Primary pain:** "We've been together forever and now we're reopening questions we thought were settled. I don't know how to hold all of this."

**Act:** 1 on entry, 2 quickly

**Feature that serves them best:** The pre/post session processing cards. For this user, the conversation isn't the problem — they've been having hard conversations for a decade. The problem is processing what comes up after. The post-session card ("What actually happened? What surprised us?") does therapeutic work the app would never claim.

**Path through the product:** Coupled Curious roadmap → rapid progression to Stage 4–6 → Desire Map → Act 2 operational tools as they start practicing

**Why they stay:** This couple has more history to protect, which means more investment in doing this right. The Agreements Vault becomes genuinely important to them — they need a place to store what they've decided because their decisions carry real weight. The longitudinal data (their check-ins, their pulse trends) becomes evidence of how the relationship is holding.

---

### Established Couple, Mid-NM, Hitting Recurring Friction

**Who:** Has been practicing ENM for 1–3 years. Past the initial curiosity stage, past the first experiences, now in the territory where patterns emerge. Specific recurring problems: scheduling stress, NRE imbalance, agreements that need renegotiation, jealousy that keeps showing up around the same trigger.

**Primary pain:** "We keep having the same conversation. We resolve it and then it comes back. We don't know how to break the pattern."

**Act:** 2

**Feature that serves them best:** The Insight Engine (when live) and Jealousy Mapping. The Insight Engine can surface: "You tend to log high anxiety on days Partner A has a date with Connection B. That pattern has appeared 7 times in 90 days." The Jealousy Mapping tool helps decode what the recurring feeling is actually about.

**Path through the product:** Possibly arrives via Act 1 word of mouth or downloads directly for Act 2 tools. Onboards into Coupled Experienced path. Skips beginner content. Goes directly to operational infrastructure.

**Why they stay:** The Insight Engine is the core retention mechanic for this user. The data compounds faster for them because the patterns are already present — they just haven't been made visible yet. Once they can see the pattern, leaving means losing the only longitudinal record that could help them address it.

---

### Established Couple, Experienced Practitioners, Wanting Better Infrastructure

**Who:** 3–10+ years of ENM practice. Possibly swinging, possibly polyamorous, possibly relationship anarchist in practice. They've built their own systems over the years. They found the app through a recommendation and downloaded skeptically.

**Primary pain:** "We've been doing this for years in Google Docs and memory. It works but it's fragile. One hard month and the informal agreements get lost."

**Act:** 2 — Arrives as word-of-mouth from experienced community

**Feature that serves them best:** The Agreements Vault and Connection Cards. The first time they can store an agreement in the app and see it sitting next to the connection it applies to — that's the recognition moment.

**Path through the product:** Downloads for the mutual reveal mechanic out of curiosity. Discovers operational infrastructure. Starts logging daily pulse because it's easier than their current system. Within 30 days, the app has replaced their Google Doc.

**Why they stay:** They've transferred their infrastructure to the app. Leaving now means rebuilding it elsewhere.

---

### Solo Person, Newly NM-Curious, No Partner Yet

**Who:** Single, NM-curious, doing the preparation work before they're in a relationship. May have read books. May have had one relationship that wasn't explicitly NM but that surfaced their interest. Is taking this seriously before it's urgent.

**Primary pain:** "I want to be ready when the time comes. I don't want to figure this out in the middle of a relationship when the stakes are high."

**Act:** 3 — Architecture present at V1.0, marketed at V1.2

**Feature that serves them best:** The Solo Desire Map completion and the Solo Curious roadmap. Both serve the "preparation before practice" use case.

**Path through the product:** Solo onboarding → Solo Curious roadmap → Desire Map solo completion → self-reflection tools → when a connection arrives, transitions to paired use

**Why they stay:** The preparation investment. They've logged their preferences, their curiosity categories, their anxieties. That record is the starting point for any new relationship. Losing it means starting over.

---

### Solo Poly Person, Established Practice, Multiple Connections

**Who:** Has been solo poly for years. Has 2–4 active connections, each at different stages. Manages significant relational complexity with no primary partner as anchor.

**Primary pain:** "The complexity is real and mostly invisible. Each connection thinks they understand my bandwidth. None of them can see the full picture. I can barely see the full picture."

**Act:** 3 — But many arrive informally in Act 2 via word of mouth

**Feature that serves them best:** Connection Cards and the Polycule Network Visualizer (when built). The ability to see all active connections with their individual histories, the agreements attached to each, the emotional state of each — this is a genuinely novel capability.

**Path through the product:** Solo Experienced roadmap → Connection Cards as primary organizing tool → Jealousy Mapping and Pulse data per connection → eventually Insight Engine showing cross-connection patterns

**Why they stay:** The network effect within their own relational graph. The more connections they log, the more the app can show them about how they move across their network. Leaving means losing the map.

---

### Person in a Situationship — Ambiguous Connection, No Label

**Who:** Has something real with someone but the structure isn't named. Might be moving toward something. Might be stuck in ambiguity by design. The uncertainty is the condition, not a temporary state.

**Primary pain:** "I don't know how to think about this clearly because there's no framework that applies. It's not a relationship and it's not nothing."

**Act:** 3 — Belongs, even if they don't identify as ENM

**Feature that serves them best:** Solo Reflection tools and the private journal. A place to process ambiguity without having to name it first.

**Path through the product:** Solo path, possibly not self-identifying as ENM. Uses journaling and reflection tools. May or may not use the Desire Map. The product doesn't require them to claim an identity.

**Why they stay:** The product is the only space that doesn't demand they resolve the ambiguity first. It holds the complexity without judgment, which is exactly what this user cannot find anywhere else.

---

### Person Whose Partner Is Not on the App

**Who:** Has a partner, possibly open, possibly not. Downloads the app individually. Might use it for solo self-work, might gradually invite their partner in.

**Primary pain:** "I want to do this work but my partner isn't interested in an app. I don't want to wait for them."

**Act:** 1 or 3 depending on relationship context

**Feature that serves them best:** The solo reflection tools and individual roadmap content. The Desire Map solo completion becomes particularly meaningful — it's the thing they can do on their own and share with a partner when the partner is ready, even if they never open the app themselves.

**Path through the product:** Solo onboarding → individual roadmap work → gradual invitation of partner when trust is established. The partner-invite flow should be frictionless — it should feel like sharing something you made, not recruiting someone to a product.

**Why they stay:** The solo work has value independent of whether the partner joins. And the product creates natural invitation moments without pressuring either party.

---

### Couple Where One Partner Uses the App and Gradually Brings the Other In

**Who:** Similar to above but the non-app partner eventually joins. The app was the first partner's territory; the second partner is entering someone else's space.

**Primary pain (for the joining partner):** "This was their thing. I feel like I'm entering a conversation that started without me."

**Act:** 1

**Feature that serves them best:** The fresh-start framing in the couple onboarding. The joining partner should never feel like they're reading their partner's diary. The mutual reveal is mutual — both partners complete it independently, regardless of when they joined.

**Path through the product:** One partner completes solo roadmap work. Partner joins. Both complete individual assessments and Desire Maps independently. Mutual reveal happens only when both are ready.

**Why they stay:** The shared artifact that builds from their first session together. The history starts when they both arrive, not before.

---

### Person Who Tried NM, It Didn't Work, Processing What Happened

**Who:** Attempted NM — might have been a few months, might have been years. It ended: the relationship ended, or the NM structure ended, or both. Now processing. Not necessarily ready to try again.

**Primary pain:** "I need to understand what happened. I don't want to carry this forward without making sense of it."

**Act:** 2 or 3 depending on circumstance

**Feature that serves them best:** The Solo Reflection tools and the Coupled Experienced "Repair Shop" roadmap stage (for couples who went through this together). The journaling tools serve the processing use case even without an active relationship.

**Path through the product:** Solo onboarding → reflection-focused content → possible transition back to partnered use if/when they're ready

**Why they stay:** The act of processing is itself valuable. The longitudinal record of how their thinking evolves after the ending is something no other product can provide.

**Note:** This is also a user type with heightened sensitivity to being pathologized. The product's voice — "every outcome, including deciding this isn't for us, is valid" — must be fully present for this user. Their ending is not a failure state.

---

### Relationship Anarchist — Resists All Labels, Wants Self-Reflection Tools

**Who:** Explicitly rejects hierarchical relationship structures and often the vocabulary of polyamory. "Relationship anarchy" may or may not be their label — more likely they just know they don't fit the standard frameworks. They don't have a "primary." They resist the infrastructure of rules and agreements. And yet they want to think carefully about how they relate.

**Primary pain:** "Every ENM resource assumes I want to structure my relationships. I want to think about them without being told how to organize them."

**Act:** 3

**Feature that serves them best:** The reflection and journaling tools, and any content that doesn't prescribe structure. The Desire Map works for them only if it's framed as self-knowledge rather than partner-compatibility-optimization.

**Path through the product:** They arrive with resistance. They stay if the product doesn't require them to adopt a framework. The content voice — which never prescribes structure, only surfaces questions — must earn their trust quickly.

**Why they stay:** Ironically, they often have the most sophisticated relational thinking of any user type. When the product meets that sophistication rather than trying to teach them, they become the most engaged users. They're also the most vocal in communities — word of mouth from a relationship anarchist who genuinely endorses the product reaches a disproportionate number of skeptics.

## 3. Market Expansion Logic

### Why the Order Matters

The sequence is a trust-building argument, not just a business decision. An app that claims on launch day to serve new couples, experienced practitioners, solo poly people, and relationship anarchists simultaneously is an app that serves no one particularly well. The claim is too broad to feel specific to any of them.

Act 1 first creates the credibility that makes Acts 2 and 3 plausible. The logic: if the product genuinely serves the hardest moment (couple in first exploration, high stakes, high anxiety), it can credibly claim to serve people doing this with more experience and more confidence. The hard problem demonstrates capability. The easy problems feel earned.

There's also a community logic. ENM communities are small and interconnected. A product that lands well with new couples gets talked about in the spaces where experienced practitioners congregate. The word-of-mouth pipeline from Act 1 to Act 2 is organic, not engineered, if Act 1 is done right.

---

### Act 1 Market

**Who:** Couples in the moment of first serious exploration. Self-identified as NM-curious. In a committed relationship. Not yet practicing.

**Size estimate:** Approximately 9–12M Americans are in or have recently been in the specific Act 1 moment (based on Haupert et al. 2017 estimates of consensual non-monogamy prevalence × the proportion who are in the "curious but not yet practicing" stage). The addressable subset — people who would download and pay for a structured tool — is probably 3–5% of that, putting the near-term TAM at 270K–600K potential paid users in the US alone.

**Use case duration:** Episodic. The acute curiosity moment lasts 3–18 months on average. After that, users either move into practice (Act 2) or don't. This means Act 1 requires continuous new user acquisition to sustain revenue, which is why Acts 2 and 3 — which have longer ongoing use cases — are existentially important to the business, not just strategically appealing.

**Conversion story:** Acute problem → onboarding normalization (stat screen) → personalized path → Desire Map (instant specific result) → paywall on that result. The conversion happens at peak intent, which is the product's strongest mechanic. The challenge is that intent is highest at moment of first curiosity and declines if acquisition takes too long.

---

### Act 2 Market

**Who:** People already practicing CNM/ENM in any form. Swinging couples, polyamorous networks, people with open agreements, people with ongoing situationships that have been informally structured. By Haupert et al. 2017 estimates, approximately 4–5% of Americans are currently practicing CNM, which suggests roughly 10–13M people.

**Size relative to Act 1:** Larger total pool, but not all of them are in a moment of acute pain that drives download motivation. The Act 2 user needs to encounter the product (usually via recommendation) and have a recognition moment ("this is what I've been missing") rather than solving an immediate crisis.

**LTV advantage:** Act 2 users have ongoing use cases that don't end when the exploration phase does. A couple doing ENM for five years has five years of potential pulse data, five years of agreement iterations, five years of jealousy logs. The LTV ceiling is dramatically higher. An Act 2 user who logs daily for a year is likely worth 5–8x an Act 1 user who churns after the curiosity phase ends.

**Conversion story:** Recognition, not urgency. The hook is not "I have a problem right now" but "I have been cobbling together a solution for years and this is a better version of it." The Drop Box, the Agreements Vault, the Insight Engine — these need to land as recognition experiences, not as learning experiences. Act 2 users know what they're doing. The product needs to meet that knowledge.

---

### Act 3 Market

**Who:** Solo people doing intentional relational work — solo poly practitioners, NM-curious singles, relationship anarchists, people navigating complex connection networks without a primary partner.

**Size:** This is likely the largest underserved segment. Many people doing this work don't identify as "ENM people." They're just people who want to be thoughtful about relationships. The addressable market expands significantly when the identity requirement is removed. The Act 3 tagline — "for people who take relationships seriously, all kinds of relationships" — intentionally does not require ENM self-identification.

**The latent demand problem:** Solo users are underserved partly because they're harder to see. Couples in crisis have an event (the first conversation) that creates a download moment. Experienced practitioners have a community that creates recommendation pathways. Solo people are more diffuse — they're in a dozen different communities or no community at all. Discovery requires a different kind of marketing: organic SEO, long-form content, word-of-mouth from people who identify with the reframe rather than the practice.

**Conversion story:** Belonging. Not urgency, not recognition — belonging. The Act 3 user has never fully fit into couple-centric ENM resources or generic self-help resources. The product's claim that this was always for them too is a genuinely emotional conversion moment if delivered right. The conversion mechanic is the Desire Map solo mode: completing it privately, getting a self-knowledge summary, having something tangible to hold. Not a paywall on someone else's reveal — a paywall on your own clarity.

## 4. Features That Unlock With Scale

These features are not blocked by engineering complexity. They're blocked by prerequisites that don't exist at V1.0 launch. Building them too early produces empty features that create false signals and erode trust. Building them too late means leaving value on the table. The signals below are the triggers.

---

### Insight Engine — Pattern Surfacing

**What it is:** Surfaces observations about a user's logged data. Not recommendations, not diagnoses — observations. "You tend to log high anxiety on Sundays. You've logged compersion 12 times, most often after check-ins rather than after dates."

**Prerequisite:** Minimum 30 days of logged data per user. Ideally 90+ days. Without sufficient data, the Insight Engine surfaces trivial patterns that undermine its credibility. A pattern spotted in 4 data points is not a pattern.

**Act:** 2

**Signal to build:** Median Act 2 user reaches 30 days of logged pulse data. At that point, the first cohort can receive insights that are actually grounded.

**Critical design constraint:** Every observation must be followed by a question, never a recommendation. "You tend to feel X after Y — what does that tell you?" The mirror framing is non-negotiable. If the Insight Engine ever sounds like it knows what the user should do, it has crossed the line from observation to prescription.

---

### Anonymous Community Feed

**What it is:** A context-mapped social layer where users share experiences and find resonance without social graph exposure. Structured to surface durable insight over ephemeral posts. (Full design: see Section 22 of PROJECT_SCOPE.md.)

**Prerequisite:** (1) Critical mass of users in overlapping emotional/relational contexts — approximately 50K+ MAU to produce meaningful clustering. (2) Moderation architecture fully designed and staffed before launch. (3) Trust established with user base about what the community is and isn't — not a forum, not a support group, not a dating pool.

**Act:** 2/3

**Signal to build:** When the Pulse and logging features have enough users that the anonymous feed would have content on day 1. Launching a community feature into silence is worse than not launching it at all. The moment someone posts and gets zero resonance, the feature feels broken.

**Critical design constraint:** The pre-post mapping flow (what kind of thing is this? → surface existing posts → compose only if nothing matches) must be built before the feed goes live. A comment box attached to a timeline is not the vision.

---

### Annual Retrospective (Working Title: "Your Year, Lightly")

**What it is:** A cinematic scroll through a year of logged data — tone read, milestone cards, emotional arc, connection summaries. Designed artifacts, not database printouts. (Full concept: see Section 23 of PROJECT_SCOPE.md.)

**Prerequisite:** (1) 6+ months of active logging per user (not installs — active logging). (2) Sufficient data diversity — pulse, jealousy logs, connection cards, and session history all need to be populated. A user who only logged pulse for six months gets a narrow retrospective that doesn't represent the full vision. (3) Design and engineering capacity to produce a genuinely cinematic experience — this is not a feature to half-build.

**Act:** 2/3

**Signal to build:** The first cohort of users reaches 6 months of active multi-feature logging. This cohort will be small initially — perhaps a few hundred users — but they are the proof of concept for the feature's emotional impact.

**Name note:** "Your Year, Lightly" is a working title. The name matters because this is an experience as much as a feature. The name should feel like a gift, not a report. Final name decision deferred until the feature is close enough to building that the right name becomes obvious from the experience itself.

---

### Therapist and Coach Mode

**What it is:** An anonymized aggregate view for ENM-affirming therapists and coaches. A therapist whose clients use Open Lightly could, with explicit client consent, see pattern summaries — not individual logs, never private entries — that help them understand where the client's relational work is happening.

**Prerequisite:** (1) Sufficient user base that therapists have reason to recommend the app. The recommendation pathway is: therapist discovers app through client, sees its value, recommends it to other clients, eventually explores whether a professional integration exists. This pathway requires Act 1 and Act 2 to have established credibility first. (2) A clear consent architecture where the client explicitly controls what, if anything, is shared with a practitioner. (3) Legal review — HIPAA adjacent but not HIPAA, since Open Lightly is not a health app. The legal framing matters.

**Act:** 2/3

**Signal to build:** Enough inbound from ENM-affirming therapists and coaches that the demand is clearly there. This is a pull feature, not a push feature — it should be built in response to demonstrated practitioner interest, not ahead of it.

**Strategic value:** The therapist recommendation is the highest-trust acquisition channel available. An ENM-affirming therapist who recommends Open Lightly to every relevant client is worth more than any paid acquisition channel at equivalent scale.

### Polycule Network Visualizer

**What it is:** A visual map of a user's full relational network — connections, connections' connections (with appropriate privacy limits), the emotional and structural nature of each relationship.

**Prerequisite:** (1) Populated Connection Cards roster — minimum 3+ active connections with logged histories. Without a populated roster, the visualizer is a map of one connection, which is not meaningfully different from a profile. (2) The design must handle significant structural variation — hierarchical poly networks, non-hierarchical, solo poly networks, kitchen table structures — without privileging one model over another.

**Act:** 3

**Signal to build:** A meaningful percentage of Act 3 users (solo poly) has 3+ active Connection Cards populated. At that point, the visualizer has something to visualize.

**Critical design constraint:** The visualizer must never imply hierarchy where the user hasn't claimed it. The default visual should be non-hierarchical. Users who practice hierarchical polyamory can configure it — it should not be assumed.

### Relationship Health Score Over Time

**What it is:** A longitudinal score (or index) derived from check-in data, pulse responses, and session frequency — showing not where a relationship is right now but how it has been trending over weeks and months.

**Prerequisite:** (1) Minimum 90 days of check-in data for the score to reflect meaningful trend rather than noise. (2) Both partners logging (for coupled users) — a health score based on one partner's data is one partner's perception, not shared health. (3) Significant care in framing — this feature is closest to the therapy line and must be positioned as a mirror, never an evaluation.

**Act:** 2

**Signal to build:** The Coupled Experienced user cohort reaches a 90-day logging median. The feature should be validated with this cohort before broad release — the framing will need iteration based on how real users interpret a score about their relationship.

### Paired Insight Engine

**What it is:** Both partners receive pattern summaries independently — generated from their own logs — and then see a synthesis of where their patterns converge or diverge. Not "you argue more than your partner" — observations about structural convergence. "You both tend to log connection spikes after long solo weekends. You both tend to log anxiety spikes in the week before major schedule changes."

**Prerequisite:** (1) Both partners logging independently for 90+ days. (2) Single-user Insight Engine already live and validated — the paired version is a second-order feature. (3) Consent architecture for cross-partner data synthesis — both partners explicitly opt in to seeing synthesized patterns. Individual logs remain private by default.

**Act:** 2

**Signal to build:** Sufficient coupled users have reached 90+ days of independent logging that the paired synthesis has enough pairs to validate against.

## 5. The Compounding Data Thesis

### The Core Argument

Every check-in, journal entry, jealousy log, and daily pulse is a data point about a person's relational life. Each individual data point has immediate value — the jealousy log is useful in the moment it's written. The daily pulse is useful as a self-check. The reflection prompt is useful as a thinking tool. This immediate utility is necessary to create the logging habit.

But the immediate value is not the product's durable advantage. The durable advantage is what happens when those data points accumulate.

The value curve is not linear. It crosses thresholds:

**Day 1 — Moment value:** A single jealousy log is useful the moment it's written. It helps decode an emotion that would otherwise just be experienced and then forgotten. This is the entry point.

**Day 30 — Pattern emergence:** A month of logs reveals weekly patterns that are invisible in single data points. "I log anxiety more on Thursdays" is a discovery only available to someone with 30 data points. The Insight Engine can start doing something real at this threshold.

**Day 90 — Seasonal and relational triggers:** Three months of data reveals patterns that are invisible at one month. "My jealousy logs spike in weeks when Partner A has multiple external obligations" is a pattern that requires 90 days of co-occurring data to see. The relationship between their schedule and your emotional state — this is genuinely novel self-knowledge.

**Day 180 — The emotional arc of a relationship's evolution:** Six months of data captures how a relationship changes over time. The early nervousness, the first signs of ease, the friction that appeared around Month 3, the way the check-ins shifted in quality after a hard conversation in Month 5. This is a longitudinal record that no therapist's notes can fully capture because therapists see clients once a week at most — they're sampling a timeline, not recording it continuously.

**Day 365 — An irreplaceable artifact:** A year of logged relational data is unlike anything a person has ever had before. It is a detailed account of how they moved through the emotional terrain of their relational life over a full year — the seasonal patterns, the relational triggers, the growth that happened so gradually it was invisible until it's mapped. The "Your Year, Lightly" retrospective becomes possible because the data is there. This artifact cannot be recreated. Leaving means losing the only record of a year of emotional experience.

### Why the Compounding Curve Matters Strategically

The inflection points above correspond to the moments when leaving the product becomes increasingly costly — not because of artificial lock-in but because of genuine accumulated self-knowledge. This is the Flo parallel.

Flo's genius was not the period tracker. Period trackers existed for decades. Flo's genius was understanding that fertility and cycle data compounds in value over time in ways that make switching costs feel real. After two years of Flo data, your cycle history, your symptom patterns, your predictive accuracy — these things feel like they belong to you but exist only within Flo's record. Leaving means starting the record over.

Open Lightly is doing this for relational life. The difference is that what compounds here is not physiological data but emotional data — harder to quantify, slower to accumulate visible patterns, but ultimately more meaningful and more personally irreplaceable.

There is an ethical dimension to this that must be named. The compounding data thesis is a retention mechanism. It works because leaving has real costs. This is only acceptable if two conditions are met: (1) the data genuinely belongs to the user and is fully exportable, and (2) the reason staying is valuable is because the data is genuinely useful, not because the product has engineered artificial dependency. The moment the compounding curve is maintained through friction rather than through genuine value, it becomes a trap. The goal is retention through irreplaceability, not retention through lock-in.

### The Identity Shift

There's a qualitative shift that happens around the 90–180 day mark that goes beyond pattern recognition. At that point, a user's logged data has become a form of self-knowledge that is more accurate than their own memory.

Memory is reconstructive. It smooths, distorts, and reinterprets. Emotional memories in particular are unreliable — people consistently misremember how they felt about events in light of how they subsequently turned out. A person who went through a difficult NM experience and came out the other side often can't accurately reconstruct what the fear felt like at the beginning, because the present-day equanimity colors the memory.

The logged record doesn't reconstruct. The jealousy log from Day 12 is the jealousy log from Day 12. The pulse response from the week of the hardest conversation is the pulse response from that week. The user who has logged for six months has access to a version of themselves that their own memory would otherwise soften or distort.

This is the thing that makes "this app knows me better than I know myself" true — not that the app is intelligent, but that it has been paying attention when the user's memory was editing.

## 6. Expansion Risks — What Could Go Wrong

### Act 1 Risks

**Churn after the use case completes**

The Act 1 use case has a natural end. Couples either move into practice (and graduate to Act 2) or don't (and leave). The risk is that churn at the Act 1 completion point is so high that Act 2 tools never get a chance to retain users.

Mitigation: The transition moment needs to be designed, not discovered. When a couple completes the Coupled Curious roadmap, the product should recognize this explicitly and surface the Act 2 path as a natural next chapter — not a separate product but a deeper level of the same product. The framing: "You've done the foundational work. Here's what sustains it."

**App Store categorization risk**

An NM exploration app in the App Store faces real categorization ambiguity. If Apple's reviewers read the product as a sex app, it gets restricted to 17+ and loses discoverability. If they read it as a relationship tools app, it competes in a broader, more accessible category.

The mitigation is in the framing at every App Store touchpoint: the name (Open Lightly — not explicit), the screenshots (conversation cards and reflection tools, not intimacy maps), the description (structured conversation tool for couples exploring ENM, not a kink discovery app). The product is accurate at every point; it just leads with the most defensible framing.

The risk doesn't fully go away. Apple's review process is inconsistent and occasionally punitive toward apps that deal with sexuality even when handled responsibly. This is a monitoring risk rather than a design risk — it requires ongoing attention rather than a one-time fix.

**The couples who use it and decide NM isn't for them**

This is not a failure state. It must be documented as a success state.

A couple who uses Open Lightly, has the conversations, does the Desire Map, and concludes that they want to remain monogamous has used the app exactly as intended. They arrived with a question they couldn't answer on their own, they did the work, and they got a clear answer. The product served them. The fact that the answer was "not for us" doesn't diminish the value of the clarity.

This framing matters for product design (the "Every outcome is valid" voice must be genuine, not performative), for marketing (the product sells the process, not the outcome), and for App Store reviews (users who found clarity through deciding not to pursue NM should be as satisfied as users who did).

**The risk of not naming this explicitly:** If the product is not clear internally that a "no" outcome is valid, the content design will subtly optimize for "yes" outcomes — which means the hesitant partner gets pressure from the app itself, not just from their partner. That would be a genuine harm.

---

### Act 2 Risks

**Experienced practitioners find the onboarding patronizing**

An experienced practitioner who downloads the app encounters onboarding designed primarily for Act 1 users. The stat screen, the normalization framing, the "you're not alone" positioning — all of this reads differently to someone who has been doing ENM for seven years. They don't need to be normalized. They need to be respected.

Mitigation: The onboarding path for users who select "experienced" at the mode select screen should move faster, skip the hand-holding content, and surface operational tools earlier. The tone calibration that happens at onboarding (the persona filter) must produce a genuinely different experience for experienced users, not just slightly different content in the same patronizing structure.

The tell that this is working: an experienced practitioner who finishes onboarding should feel like the app read them correctly, not like they sat through an intro lecture they didn't need.

**Feature bloat — adding operational tools without losing the emotional core**

The risk of Act 2 is that the product becomes a relationship management system — a CRM for your partners — and loses the emotional intelligence that makes Act 1 compelling. Agreements Vault and Connection Cards are infrastructure. Infrastructure is not the product. The product is the structured reflection, the conversation tools, the mutual transparency mechanics. If users start experiencing Open Lightly primarily as a relationship database rather than a relationship intelligence tool, the emotional differentiation erodes.

Mitigation: Every Act 2 feature should be evaluated against the question "does this bring users closer to self-knowledge and mutual understanding, or does it just organize information?" The Agreements Vault earns its place because agreements that are stored and reviewed are agreements that are actually honored — the operational tool serves the relational goal. The moment a feature is purely logistical with no relational value, it shouldn't ship.

**The product identity blur**

"Are you a learning app or a tracking app?" is the external version of this risk. Internally it's: "Are we building for the moment of insight or for ongoing management?" The answer is both, but the balance matters. If Act 2 tools dominate the experience, new Act 1 users — who are the top of the acquisition funnel — encounter a product that feels more like a relationship operating system than a conversation starter. That changes what the product means to them, and not in a way that serves the front-door conversion.

Mitigation: The Act 2 tools should be discoverable but not dominant. They live in the depth of the product — after the initial roadmap work, after the Desire Map, after the first few sessions. They're the next room, not the entrance.

---

### Act 3 Risks

**Solo users feeling like guests in a couples app**

The single biggest risk for Act 3 is that solo users encounter a product that has coupled-user assumptions baked in at every level — language that assumes a partner, features that only make sense in pairs, a structural orientation toward the dyad. If this is the experience, solo users leave before they've found the value.

Mitigation: A content audit before Act 3 marketing launches, focused specifically on finding and replacing coupled-assumption language throughout the product. This is not about adding a solo mode — it's about removing the architectural assumption of couplehood from the base experience. The product should be genuinely neutral by default, with coupled and solo tools both present and equally accessible.

**The Desire Map solo mode feeling incomplete**

A solo user completing the Desire Map gets a self-knowledge summary. There's no mutual reveal — there's no partner. The risk is that they complete the assessment and feel like they've done the set-up work for a feature they can't access yet. That's a frustrating experience.

Mitigation: The solo Desire Map completion must be reframed as valuable in itself, not as a first step toward something else. The self-knowledge summary should be substantive and specific — not a teaser for the mutual reveal but a genuine artifact of self-understanding. "Here's what you know clearly. Here's where you're still exploring. Here's what you'd want a partner to know when the time comes." That framing treats the solo completion as a complete experience rather than a waiting room.

**The accidental dating app signal**

Marketing to solo NM-curious people risks being read as "this will help you find ENM partners." That's not what the product is. Open Lightly is not a dating app. It does not match people. It does not have a social graph. If the marketing or the App Store copy implies connection-finding, the wrong users download and the right users don't.

Mitigation: The Act 3 marketing must be explicit about what the product is not. "Not a dating app. Not a community finder. A self-knowledge tool for people doing the work of intentional non-monogamy — with or without a partner." The clarity of the negative claim is as important as the clarity of the positive one.

## 7. The Product in 5 Years

### What It Is, If Everything Works

Five years from now, Open Lightly is the first thing ENM-adjacent people reach for when their relational life becomes complex. Not because it's the only option, but because the four and a half years they've already been using it have produced something they can't find anywhere else: a continuous record of how they actually move through the emotional terrain of their relationships.

A user who has logged for five years has something unprecedented. They have a longitudinal record of who they were relationally at the beginning — the anxieties, the excitement, the misaligned assumptions — and the documentation of how they changed. They can trace the evolution of a specific connection from the first check-in to where it is now. They can see the pattern of how jealousy has appeared and shifted over years of practice. They have the actual emotional arc of their relational development, not their reconstructed memory of it.

This is the "relationship intelligence layer" from the project scope, fully realized. Not a dashboard of current metrics. A personal archive of relational development over time — accurate, portable, and irreplaceable.

### What the Product Knows About a Five-Year User

If the compounding data thesis proves out:

- Their relational patterns across dozens of significant emotional events, with clear signals about what triggers anxiety, what produces compersion, what structural conditions support their best relational functioning
- The history of every active and closed connection, including the agreements made, revised, and retired over time
- The seasonal and structural patterns of their relational bandwidth — when they expand, when they contract, what external conditions affect their emotional availability
- The evolution of their preferences and clarity — how the Desire Map would look different today than it did four years ago, which areas have clarified and which have remained genuinely ambiguous
- The emotional arc of their most significant relationships, including how they recovered from difficult periods and what that recovery looked like in the data

No therapist has this view. A good therapist who has worked with someone for five years has session notes and their clinical impressions. They've sampled the timeline. They haven't recorded it continuously.

### The Therapist Integration

By Year 5, the product has established enough credibility in ENM-affirming clinical communities that some therapists and coaches are recommending it as homework. Not as a substitute for therapy — the Moral Red Line is permanent — but as a complement. The way some therapists recommend journaling, some will recommend Open Lightly logging.

The integration opportunity: a user brings their Open Lightly data to a therapy session the way someone brings a Flo health report to a doctor. Not the raw logs — never that — but a synthesized summary: pattern insights, trend data, the emotional arc of the period in question. The therapist sees what the data has revealed; the user has something substantive to bring to the conversation.

This integration does not require the product to become clinical. It requires the product to produce synthesized summaries that are legible to practitioners. The Insight Engine, at full maturity, is capable of producing these. The export is patient-controlled, consent-explicit, and designed for therapeutic context rather than clinical diagnosis.

The strategic value: an ENM-affirming therapist who integrates Open Lightly into their practice becomes a continuous acquisition channel. Every relevant client they see is a potential user. This is the highest-trust, lowest-CAC acquisition pathway available, and it only becomes possible after the product has established clinical credibility through years of responsible use.

### The Annual Retrospective at Maturity

Five years in, the annual retrospective ("Your Year, Lightly" or whatever it ultimately becomes) is the product's signature cultural moment. The experience: once a year, usually in January, a user receives a cinematic scroll through their relational year. It opens with a tone read derived from their logged data. It surfaces the moments that defined the year — the first time they logged compersion after a period of logging mostly anxiety, the connection that deepened, the agreement they made and actually kept, the month their bandwidth was stretched and how they navigated it.

Each significant moment is presented as a designed postcard — something beautiful enough to feel like the year deserved it. The postcard can be kept private, shared with a specific partner, or (without identifying information) contributed to the anonymous community feed as a moment that might resonate with someone else.

The product at maturity knows that a year of relational life is an achievement. It treats it that way.

### The Community Layer at Critical Mass

Five years in, the anonymous community feed is not a forum. It's an indexed body of collective relational experience organized by emotional context, relational stage, and structural configuration. It functions the way a great library functions — you go there not to post but to find yourself in what's already there.

When the feed has critical mass:

- A user in their third month of a new connection, experiencing something that feels like NRE anxiety, finds a post from someone who was in exactly that position two years ago and wrote about what it actually felt like and how it resolved. Not advice — experience. The difference matters.
- A user navigating a relationship anarchist network finds perspectives from people who have built the same kind of structure and can describe what the friction points actually are, not what they theoretically might be.
- A solo poly person planning their first conversation with a new potential connection finds documentation of how other solo poly people have had that conversation, with enough context to actually be useful.

The feed is the thing Reddit can never be: a place where people arrive with language and self-knowledge the product gave them, and where the collective knowledge compounds rather than resets with every new thread.

### The One-Sentence Vision

The product earns this tagline when a solo poly person who has never been in a primary partnership, an experienced swinger who has been doing this for a decade with their spouse, and a couple in their third month of first exploration all open Open Lightly on the same day and each of them feels, unambiguously, that the product was made for them.

> *"For people who take relationships seriously. All kinds of relationships."*

That claim is not marketing copy. It's a description of what the product actually is — when it's finished.
