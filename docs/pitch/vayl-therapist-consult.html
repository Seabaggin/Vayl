01Why this exists

The population is large. The infrastructure isn't.

Consensual non‑monogamy is common enough to be a cross‑section of the population, not a subculture, yet almost nothing in mainstream relationship tech, or mainstream therapy intake, is built assuming a client might be one of them.

02The line I don't cross

Vayl is not a clinical or assessment tool. This is the design constraint that matters most to me, and the one I most want checked.

Governing rule

Vayl gives people maps, vocabulary, and mirrors, and lets them make the determinations. It never issues a finding about a user. It can name what someone said. It never infers what they didn't say.

Not this — assessment
"You're an Explorer." / "Do you recharge alone? You're an introvert."
This — discovery
"You said you want relationships outside your main one. That's often called polyamory. Here's where to explore it."

Two operations only are permitted on a user's own answers: comparing two people's points (the Desire Map), or ranking and describing one person's own answers back to them. Every quiz ends at a door to content, never at a verdict about who they are. I'd like your read on whether that line actually holds up across the app, not just on paper.

03What's actually in the app

Four tools, one relationship

Each does one job. None of them assume the couple lives inside the app between visits.

Desire Map

Where you meet, and where there's distance

Intent

Each partner rates their own interest in ~40 structures and acts, privately, before either sees the other's answers. The reveal only ever compares two points against each other. It never characterizes either person alone, no "your profile is X," only "here's the overlap, and here's the gap worth talking through."

This is the one place in the app that performs the "compare two points" operation the discovery‑not‑assessment rule allows. It's the only assessment‑adjacent surface in Vayl, and it's built to stay strictly comparative.

Desire Map · reveal
Card Sessions

A structured, two‑device conversation, not a quiz

Intent

Couples pick a deck and move through it together, in sync, one card at a time. Decks are graded by intensity (1–10) and staged into arcs, so a couple can't stumble from small talk into a hard disclosure without the deck itself building the ground for it first.

The sample deck at right, Jealousy & What It's Telling You, is a real deck shipping in the app. Notice the sequencing: memory, then upbringing, then a body‑check (no words, just locate the sensation), and only then a context beat that reframes jealousy as signal before the card that asks what it's pointing at.

Learn

A research library that shows its limitations

Intent

Every finding in Learn carries its citation and, deliberately, a stated limitation, right in the same card as the headline stat. I did not want a "CNM is fine, studies prove it" wall. I wanted a library that models the actual epistemic humility the research itself has.

This is the section I'd most value your eye on directly: are these three findings represented fairly, and is there a foundational study or caution I'm missing that belongs alongside them before this ships to users?

Pulse

A capacity check‑in, not a mood tracker

Intent

A quick two‑axis check‑in (roughly openness × groundedness) that places each partner in one of four emotional‑capacity spaces: expansive, sovereign, reactive, protective. It's meant to answer one question honestly: is now a good time to open a hard conversation, or not. There's no streak, no daily requirement, no penalty for skipping it for a week.

04Safety scaffolding

What stops a session from going somewhere a couple didn't consent to

Immediate exit, always available

A safe word ends a session at any moment. It closes the session with a reflection option, or lets either partner leave with nothing further asked of them.

Can't be swiped away by accident

Sessions run in the app's most protected presentation mode, with interactive dismissal disabled and a confirm‑on‑exit step, the same discipline as an in‑progress lesson, not a casual sheet.

Consent to see, before consent to answer

Sensitive cards are flagged and skippable by design. Nothing in a deck is positioned as mandatory to "complete" the deck.

No manufactured urgency

No streaks, no "your partner is waiting" push pressure, no in‑app notification for a breakup. The relationship happens off‑app; Vayl doesn't compete for primacy with it.

05What I want from you

Four specific questions, not a general review

1

Does the jealousy deck's sequencing actually work clinically?

Memory, then upbringing, then a body check, then a reframe, then the harder prompt. Is that a defensible progression, or does it move too fast, or in the wrong order, for a couple in real distress?

2

Is "jealousy as signal, not a verdict" the right frame?

The app deliberately never tells a user their jealousy means something is wrong. Is that the responsible stance, or does it risk under‑naming a genuine red flag in some relationships?

3

Where does a couple in crisis need a harder off‑ramp than a safe word?

Right now the only exit from a hard conversation is ending the session. Should certain decks (jealousy, "when it gets hard") surface a referral to real‑world support instead of just closing?

4

Are the three research findings shown here represented fairly?

Haupert on prevalence, Conley on monogamy myths, Rubel & Bogaert on wellbeing parity. Full text and citations are in the app's Learn tab, they're one of ~30 findings currently seeded.

Thank you for looking at this before anyone else does.

I'd rather find out now that a card sequence or a claim needs to change than after a couple is mid‑conversation because of it.
