Pill direction 1 — Full-width vertical stack

This is SelectablePill's actual native, tested form — one option per row, full width. Sidesteps the horizontal-scroll issue entirely (nothing to scroll, no layout ambiguity), every option visible at once, and reads calmer/more deliberate — you're choosing from a short list, not hunting through a scrolling row. Costs more vertical space (5 rows vs 1), so the question sits higher and the pills push toward the bottom half.

Pill direction 2 — Segmented continuum track

Instead of five separate buttons, one continuous bar divided into five zones — visually this is what the data actually is: a position on a continuum, the same idea as the orb's own axis placement. Tapping a zone feels like "placing yourself on a line" rather than "picking an item from a menu," which fits the ceremony better than discrete chips do. Compact (one row, no scroll, no wrap), and it's a genuinely different interaction language from what's there now — worth seeing if it feels right before committing.

Pill direction 3 — Wrapped grid chips (no scroll)

Keeps the discrete-chip feel from what's there now, but wraps into rows instead of scrolling horizontally — so nothing is ever off-screen or requires a swipe to discover, without changing the visual language much. The safest "fix the mechanics, keep the vibe" option of the three.
